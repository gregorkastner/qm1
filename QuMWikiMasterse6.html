1.6 QM1 – Session 6 (Interest Rates)

Continuous compounding

In most scenarios the nominal annual interest rate is given. It is a fictional number, that is only useful when the number of interest dates per year is known. In case of one interest date per year, the nominal and the effective annual interest rate are the same. The formula for calculating the capital stock after time t is thus the following (one interest date per year):

S(t) = S(0) 1 + it

wheretis time in years,
S(t)is the capital stock at time t
andiis the nominal annual interest rate.

When there are more interest dates per year, i. e. compounding takes place n times per year, the formula looks similar:

Sn(t) = S(0) 1 + i nnt

wheretis time in years,
S(t)is the capital stock at time t,
iis the nominal annual interest rate
and n is the number of interest dates per year.

For compounding infinitely many times per year, i. e. continuous compounding, the following holds:

S∞(t) = lim n→∞Sn(t) = lim n→∞S(0) 1 + i nnt = i n = 1 m lim m→∞S(0) 1 + 1 mimt = S(0) lim m→∞1 + 1 mm︸ eit = S(0)eit

Consequently the effective annual interest rate is

r = 1 + i n n − 1forn < ∞ ei − 1forn = ∞

nomintrateyear <- 1  # nominal annual interest rate
ntimes <- c(1, 12, 360, 360*24*60*60)  # compounding n times
(effintrateyear <- (1 + nomintrateyear/ntimes)^(ntimes))  # effective annual i. r.
## [1] 2.000000 2.613035 2.714516 2.718282

exp(nomintrateyear)  # effective annual interest rate for continuous compounding
## [1] 2.718282

tyears <- 3
(effintrate <- (1 + nomintrateyear/ntimes)^(ntimes*tyears))  # eff. i. r. for t years
## [1]  8.00000 17.84168 20.00218 20.08554

exp(nomintrateyear * tyears)  # effective interest rate for continuous compounding
## [1] 20.08554

AAA-Rated Bonds

In mid-2018, the nominal annual interest rate at the European central bank for AAA-rated bonds with a maturity of 1 year was about − 0.7%. Assuming that the interest rate stays at this level and the interest is “paid” i) yearly, ii) monthly, iii) continuously, compute a) the time it takes for such a bond to lose 10% of its original value, and b) the respective effective interest rate!

–

S(t) = 0.9 ⋅ S(0) = S(0) 1 + rt 0.9 = 1 + rt ⇔ t = ln(0.9) ln(1 + r)
wheretis time in years,
S(t)is the capital stock at time t
andris the effective annual interest rate.
nomintrateyear <- -.007
ntimes <- c(1, 12, 42424242)
effintrateyear <- (1 + nomintrateyear/ntimes)^ntimes - 1
effintrateyear[4] <- exp(nomintrateyear) - 1
names(effintrateyear) <- c("yearly", "monthly", ntimes[3], "continuously")

Chris agrees to pay Pat EUR 1000 one year from now and EUR 2000 two years from now. In return, Pat pays Chris EUR 3000 three years from now. Determine the present and the future value of this contract when the interest rate is 5% (annual compounding).

ntimes <- c(1L)

Internal rate of return (IRR)

You give your trustworthy lecturere EUR 10.000, and he promises to pay back EUR 2.000 after one year, EUR 2.500 after two years, EUR 3.000 after three years, and 3.500 after four years.

Calculate the internal rate of return.

Solution 1

Roots (i. e. where is the expression equal to 0) for the following equation have to be found:

−10.000 + 2.000 (1 + r) + 2.500 (1 + r)2 + 3.000 (1 + r)3 + 3.500 (1 + r)4

which is euqal to a polinomial of degree 4 in x = 1 1+r:

−10.000 + 2.000x + 2.500x2 + 3.000x3 + 3.500x4

mypoly <- c(-10, 2, 2.5, 3, 3.5) * 1e3
(allroots <- polyroot(mypoly))
## [1]  0.9653925-0.000000i -1.4932181-0.000000i -0.1646586-1.398175i  
## [4] -0.1646586+1.398175i

typeof(allroots)
## [1] "complex"

It appears that some of these roots are complex. To get rid of these roots, the following is tried:

(realroots <- Re(allroots[Im(allroots) == 0]))  # Does not work!
## numeric(0)

#' This approach does not work, because not any root has a real 0 as imaginary part ...
#' and due to the limits of number representation in R (see 'Data' session).
(realroots <- Re(allroots[abs(Im(allroots)) < 1e-13]))  # Works perfectly!
## [1]  0.9653925 -1.4932181

#' This approch works, incorporating a tolerance from 0.
typeof(realroots)
## [1] "double"

Due to the substitution x = 1 1+r, the results have to be mapped back to r = 1 x − 1.

(effintrateyear <- 1 / realroots - 1)
## [1]  0.03584811 -1.66969453

Since the refunding is more than the initial investment, only the positive solution is meaningful. Thus, the internal rate of return of this contract is 3.5848112%.
